The anatomy of a converting page

Your homepage is not a landing page.

A homepage is a reception desk: it greets everyone and points in six directions. A landing page is a closer: one visitor, one promise, one action. Here are the six blocks, in order, with what each one is doing.

The five-second test

The top of the page must answer three questions.

Question 1

What is this?

The headline names the exact thing the ad promised. Not your brand story. The thing.

Question 2

Is it for me?

The subhead names who it's for and what situation they're in, so the right person feels seen and the wrong one leaves cheaply.

Question 3

What next?

One button, above the fold, that says exactly what happens when it's pressed.

Six blocks, in order

The page, broken down.

01

Headline matches the ad

If the ad said "remortgage", the headline says "remortgage". This is message match, the single biggest reason pages convert or don't. The subhead answers "is this for me?".

02

One action, above the fold

No menu, no footer links, no "Learn more". A page with one call to action beats one with two. The button says what happens: "Book my free call", not "Submit".

03

Proof before promises

Regulation status, reviews, market access, years in practice. People decide emotionally and justify with proof; give them the proof before the form.

04

Outcomes, not features

Not "comprehensive mortgage advice". Instead: the exact monthly difference in pounds, a clear yes or no. Specific outcomes make the call feel valuable before it's booked.

05

Kill the objections

Every visitor has a silent objection. "Will this hurt my credit score?" "Am I too early?" If the page doesn't answer them, the back button does.

06

Ask again, short form

Name and one way to reach them. Every extra field costs enquiries. Qualifying questions wait for the call. The button repeats the first one's words.

Why this matters to your budget

Same ad spend. Double the enquiries.

A page converting at 10% instead of 5% halves your cost per enquiry overnight, with zero extra ad spend. That's why I fix the page before I ever ask a client to raise the budget. It's leak number two, and almost always the cheapest to plug.
