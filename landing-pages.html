The anatomy of a converting page

Your homepage is not a landing page.

A homepage is a reception desk: it greets everyone and points in six directions. A landing page is a closer: one visitor, one promise, one action. Here are the six blocks, in order, with what each one is doing.

The five-second test

The top of the page has to answer three questions.

If a visitor can't answer these within five seconds of landing, they're gone, and the click money goes with them.

Question 1

What is this?

The headline names the exact thing the ad promised. Not your brand story. The thing.

Question 2

Is it for me?

The subhead names who it's for and what situation they're in, so the right person feels seen and the wrong one leaves cheaply.

Question 3

What do I do next?

One button, above the fold, that says exactly what happens when it's pressed.

Block 1 — The headline matches the ad

Say the thing they clicked for.

If the ad said "remortgage", the headline says "remortgage". This is called message match, and it's the single biggest reason pages convert or don't. The subhead immediately answers "is this for me?" by naming the exact situation.

Block 2 — One action, above the fold

One button. One job.

No menu, no footer links to five other pages, no "Learn more". A landing page with two calls to action converts worse than one with one. The button says what happens: "Book my free call", not "Submit".

Block 3 — Proof before promises

Borrow trust early.

Regulation status, reviews, market access, years in practice. Whatever is true and verifiable goes here. People decide emotionally and justify with proof; give them the proof before the form, not after.

Block 4 — What they actually get

Outcomes, not features.

Not "comprehensive mortgage advice". Instead: the exact monthly difference in pounds, a clear yes or no. Specific outcomes make the call feel valuable before it's booked. Vague benefits make it feel like a sales pitch.

Block 5 — Kill the objections

Answer the doubt they won't email you about.

Every visitor has one or two silent objections. "Will this hurt my credit score?" "Am I too early?" If the page doesn't answer them, the back button does. Two or three honest questions and plain answers, no accordion of twenty.

Block 6 — Ask again, with a short form

The fewer the fields, the more the leads.

Name and one way to reach them. Every extra field costs enquiries. The qualifying questions can wait for the call itself. The button repeats the same words as the first one, so the page feels like one promise from top to bottom.

Why this matters to your budget

Same ad spend. Double the enquiries.

A page that converts at 10% instead of 5% halves your cost per enquiry overnight, with zero extra ad spend. That's why I fix the page before I ever ask a client to raise the budget. It's leak number two, and it's almost always the cheapest one to plug.
